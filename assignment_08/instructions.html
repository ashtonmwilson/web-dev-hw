The 3 P's

  1. Preparedness
  2. Patience
  3. Progression

Not always in this order, these skills will come in practically handy,
and will lead to an ability to start fires in any condition.

The 3 essentials

  • Fuel
  • Oxygen
  • Heat

This may seem redundant as it is also the supplies list,
however, recognizing and maintaining a balance between theese
three things, while building the fire, is essential to success.

Getting started, Preperation:

In order to start the process we must first select a safe site,
out from under trees. Clear any burnable debris away from your fire site,
and do your best to use existing pits when available

Kneel on one knee (for faster mobility in an emergnecy) over the pit,
have your 1s,2s,3s and water within arms reach. Pull out two of the 2s,
and place them parrallel to eachother similarly to the image below, creating a trough.
Take a solid handfull of 1's and place them at the narrower end of the trough.

Lighting the fire, Patience:

Light the match, or lighter, and hold it under the bundle of elevated 1s.
Wait patiently as the 1s catch fire.

Once the ones have a flame that is peaking above them, move on to the final step.

Finding the balance, Progression:

As the pile of ones becomes a glowing ember in the center, and still has a flame
peaking, start to add the 2s slowly. Here you have some creative freedom.
Some people use a Tee Pee formation, others go with the log cabin, either will do.

What is most important is moving slowly up throught the different sizes of sticks
in order to feed the fire while increasing the heat. All the while keeping in mind
the three things a fire needs to thrive: fuel, oxygen, and heat.

While adding sticks:

  1. Leave enough room for oxygen to pass through.
  2. Keep the logs close enough to maintain heat.
  3. Add the sticks/fuel with intention, knowing where the next one will go.

Remember a healthy and balanced fire is smokeless.

SAFTEY PRECAUTIONS:

  • Always have water nearby: a hose, or full five gallon bucket.
  • Check your local laws and current fire danger levels. LNT.
  • Keep it small! If a stick is too big to break, it's too big to burn
